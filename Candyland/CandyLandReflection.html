CandyLand Reflection

For the CandyLand project we worked in a group to create a gingerbread house. Using Illustrator and the laser cutter, we designed and cut out pieces of gingerbread. We put them together, decorated it, and added electronic elements to help it come to life.

Authors: Henry Johnson(designer) Anaya Brown(Project manager), Katherine Feiner(programmer)

Link to group folder:https://drive.google.com/drive/u/0/folders/1FmdNvpDh2RZoax5AEX6hBXsGOm83ioTB 

In preparation:

In order to explore Microbit, we used code examples while the microbits were attached to our laptops in order to test the capabilities of the coding software and the microbits themselves. We also started off by looking at different types of structures like automobiles, towers, builders, and others. We ended up picking Big Ben and its surrounding building to construct.

Overview of your project/process/Challenges, setbacks, and solutions:

  • Designer/Builder: Henry

The design process started after we picked the structure that we were going to build. I started by looking at pictures of Big Ben on google images. From this, I was able to decide which pieces and details I would have to place on my file. I knew that I had to make the illustrator file 16 x 24 inches, since the gingerbread sheets would also be 16 x 24. Once I created the illustrator file, I started by outlining the cut layer. This would be the layer that the laser fully cut through to make the pieces. I added 4 large rectangles for the base of the clock tower and then added smaller shapes to make the clock face and the structures above it that make the roof. Once I finished the cut layer with all of the basic pieces of the clock tower, I decided to create the score layer. This layer was created to outline the fine details that would later be covered with icing. Along with score lines all over the different pieces of the clock, I added roman numerals onto the clock face to mimic the real tower. This is what this page looked like when I was done (right):

Once this page of the file was done, Mrs. Hedstrom and I put a 18 x 24 inch piece of cardboard in the laser cutter and cut it with this file in order to see what it looked like before using the gingerbread.  Once the pieces were cut out of the cardboard, we glued them together with hot glue in the correct way (Right). This model did not have the score lines on it.

Once we, as a group, saw how this model looked, we thought that I didn’t look great simply standing by itself. Our first big solution in this project is that we decided that it would look better and be more sturdy if we added another building to the side. I Immediately started working on this side building with the same steps as the first tower. I had to create a new page in the same file and used it as extra space to create the pieces of the building. This is what the entire file looked like with the new pieces included:

        

After getting my file approved my Mrs. Hedstrom, we used the 16 x 24 inches piece of gingerbread from the bakery inside the laser cutter to create both pages of the file. Once they came out of the laser cutter, we attempted to use X-acto knives to fully cut out the pieces from the gingerbread. The picture above and right is what the gingerbread looked like in the laser cutter. This is where we encountered our largest problem: the Gingerbread pieces breaking while cutting. This happened to multiple smaller pieces that were part of the clock tower. As a result, Mrs. Hedstrom had to print out a completely new sheet for us to cut out the pieces that we had broken. Once we had all of the pieces cut out of the gingerbread, we started building. While building, we encountered another problem - the gingerbread pieces were extremely thick and were hard to glue together. We did not have this problem with the cardboard since it was far thinner. In order to fix this, we had to use our X-acto knives to make the pieces thinner/smaller so that they could fit. Once we did this, the rest of the building process was not too difficult as we had a lot of hot glue. This is what the building looked like without icing (Right):

Once the structure was fully built, we started the decorating/icing process. We mixed oil, water, and powdered sugar in order to make a thick enough icing that would resist gravity while on the side of the building. We also cut out pieces of candy for the windows and the roofs on the side building. This process was extremely messy, but this was what the building looked like after we fully decorated it and put it on the platform(Right):

  • Programmer: Katherine

Pseudocode → Troubleshooting → Code for Micro:bit with comments

For my job as programmer, I was tasked with adding special electronic elements to our Big Ben gingerbread house. First, I used a microbit and the microbit software on my laptop to create a melody that will play when button A on the microbit is pressed. Then, I moved on to working on lighting. Originally, we planned on having LED lights that went around the base of the building. However, we ran out of LED light strips and had to find another solution. We experimented using circular formations of LED lights, but only half of the circle would light up. I was having quite a bit of trouble connecting the wires of the LED strips to the microbit. This was where I struggled the most. Eventually, we landed on a rectangular strip of 8 super-bright LED lights. I coded them so they would each be a different color in the rainbow. The lights were very bright, so we put a thin piece of paper over top the strip in order to mitigate the brightness.
